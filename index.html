Unit 1: Data

bit: A binary digit, either 0 or 1.

byte: A sequence of 8 bits.

roundoff: Error from not enough bits to represent a number precisely.

analog data: Values that change smoothly over time.

lossless: Compression that preserves all data.

lossy: Compression that discards some data.

metadata: Data about data, like descriptive info about a file.

Unit 2: Computer Systems and Networks

Computing Device: A physical device that can run a program.

Computer Network: A group of interconnected computing devices.

Path: The series of connections between devices.

Protocol: Rules for how systems communicate.

Fault Tolerant: Ability to keep working even if parts fail.

Scalability: Ability of a system to expand or contract.

Bandwidth: Max transmission capacity, measured in bits per second.

URL: Human-readable web address.

IP: Assigns unique addresses and routes data.

TCP: Ensures reliable delivery of data packets.

UDP: Faster, lightweight alternative to TCP.

Packets: Small chunks of data for transmission.

Routers: Devices that forward data across networks.

DNS: Translates domain names into IP addresses.

HTTP: Protocol for transmitting web pages.

WWW: System of linked pages and media browsable via HTTP.

Internet Censorship Around the World

Many countries restrict internet access in different ways. Below are examples of how censorship is enforced around the globe:

China

Extensive filtering with the "Great Firewall" that blocks foreign websites and services.

Iran

Government blocks many social media platforms and monitors online activity.

Russia

Increasing control over internet traffic and restrictions on political content.

North Korea

Public access to the global internet is nearly nonexistent; only a domestic intranet exists.

Saudi Arabia

Strict filtering of political, social, and religious content online.

Vietnam

Government censors political speech and restricts press freedom online.

Turkey

Frequent blocking of social media platforms and news sites, especially during political unrest.

Egypt

Authorities block messaging apps and monitor online dissent, especially during protests.

Pakistan

Frequent shutdowns of social media platforms and mobile networks during unrest.

Ethiopia

Internet blackouts are often imposed during times of conflict or political tension.

Belarus

Authorities heavily restrict access to independent news websites and social platforms.

United Arab Emirates

Blocks VoIP services and restricts political speech online, though many citizens use VPNs.
