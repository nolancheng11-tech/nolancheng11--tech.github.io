Unit 1: Data

A bit is a binary digit, either 0 or 1. Eight bits form a byte, which stores more information. Sometimes, data suffers from roundoff when there arenâ€™t enough bits to represent a number precisely. Analog data changes smoothly over time, like sound waves, while computers store digital versions. Compression can be lossless, keeping all the data, or lossy, where some detail is discarded. Files often carry metadata, which is data about data (like the author or file size).

Unit 2: Computer Systems and Networks

A computing device like a computer or smartphone connects to a computer network. Data travels along a path, following a protocol that defines communication rules. Networks are designed to be fault tolerant and support scalability. The bandwidth determines the maximum data rate. A URL gives a human-readable address. Beneath it all, the physical network carries signals, while IP assigns addresses. TCP ensures reliable delivery, while UDP is faster but less reliable. Messages are split into packets and guided by routers. The DNS translates names into IP addresses, and HTTP enables access to the World Wide Web (WWW).

Internet Censorship Around the World

Some countries restrict internet access to control information. Here are examples:

  • China: Extensive censorship through the "Great Firewall," blocking many foreign websites and filtering social media.
  • Iran: Social media platforms and many news outlets are restricted; the government monitors online activity.
  • North Korea: Citizens have access only to a tightly controlled intranet, not the global internet.
  • Russia: Increasing restrictions on news outlets, VPNs, and foreign platforms, especially during conflicts.
  • Turkey: Blocks on social media during political unrest; censorship of critical journalism.
  • Saudi Arabia: Restricts content deemed immoral or critical of the government; heavy monitoring of communication.
  • Vietnam: Limits on dissenting political content; social media monitored closely.
  • Egypt: Blocks news outlets and social media during political crises.
  • Ethiopia: Internet shutdowns during protests and exams; restricted media websites.
  • Belarus: Crackdowns on independent news; social media blocks during protests.
